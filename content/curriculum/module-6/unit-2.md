# Unit 2: Reporting and Communicating ESG Performance

## Unit 2 – Introduction

Reporting is not just about compliance, but about building trust. As stakeholders increasingly scrutinise ESG claims, organisations must communicate their performance in a clear, balanced and credible way.

Stakeholders such as investors, employees, customers, public authorities and local communities increasingly expect organisations to explain not only what they do on sustainability, but also how well they are performing and where they still face challenges.

Poor or unclear reporting can damage credibility, even when ESG actions are genuine. For this reason, organisations must learn how to communicate ESG performance in a way that is clear, balanced and transparent, avoiding exaggeration or selective storytelling.

This unit explores the principles of transparent ESG reporting, highlights common risks such as greenwashing, and supports learners in translating ESG data into meaningful messages for different audiences.

2. Communicating your ESG engagement to your audiences.

## Reporting Guidelines (EU Commission)

To ensure that ESG reporting is consistent, comparable and reliable, the European Commission has introduced common reporting principles through the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS). These principles apply to organisations required to report under the CSRD and also serve as a useful reference for voluntary reporting.

## Cross-cutting rules you must apply (ESRS 1)

One of the central concepts of ESG reporting is double materiality. This means that organisations must look at sustainability from two perspectives at the same time.

On the one hand, they must consider how their activities impact people and the environment. On the other hand, they must analyse how sustainability issues, such as climate change or social risks, affect the company’s financial performance, position and future development.

Another key principle is the reporting boundary and value chain. ESG reporting should not be limited to what happens inside the organisation itself. Companies are expected to consider material impacts, risks and opportunities along their value chain, including suppliers, logistics, customers and, where relevant, end-of-life stages. When precise data is not available, organisations should explain the methods and assumptions used.

ESG reporting also requires a time perspective. Organisations should distinguish between short-, medium- and long-term horizons, clearly state the base year for their data, and define timelines for targets. This helps stakeholders understand progress over time rather than isolated results.

Finally, all ESG information must meet key qualitative characteristics. It should be:

- complete, meaning that relevant information is not omitted;
- comparable over time and, where possible, with peers;
- clear and understandable for non-experts;
- verifiable, meaning that data and statements can be checked and supported by evidence.

## Minimum disclosures that apply to all reporters (ESRS 2)

In addition to general principles, ESRS requires organisations to disclose a minimum set of information regardless of sector.

First, organisations must explain governance arrangements related to ESG. This includes who is responsible for oversight, which roles exist, and whether those involved have the appropriate expertise.

Second, organisations must describe how ESG is integrated into their strategy. This means explaining the business model, how resilient it is to sustainability-related risks, and how impacts, risks and opportunities influence strategic decisions and resource allocation.

Third, organisations must explain how they identify and manage impacts, risks and opportunities (IROs). This includes due diligence processes, stakeholder engagement and internal management systems.

Finally, organisations must disclose metrics and targets. These should be time-bound and outcome-oriented, and organisations should clearly explain how the metrics are calculated and what assumptions are used.

## Topical standards

Beyond general disclosures, ESRS includes topical standards that apply only if a topic is considered material.

Environmental topics include:

- climate change;
- pollution;
- water and marine resources;
- biodiversity;
- resource use and circular economy.

Social topics cover issues related to:

- the workforce;
- workers in the value chain;
- affected communities;
- consumers or end-users.

Governance topics focus on business conduct.

A key logic under ESRS is that organisations should disclose information in a structured way, moving from policies, to actions and resources, to targets, and finally to metrics. If a topic is not material, it should not be disclosed under the topical standard.

ESG disclosures must also be prepared for digital tagging and may be subject to assurance, reinforcing the need for accuracy and internal consistency.

## Risks of misleading or incomplete reporting

Even when organisations act in good faith, ESG reporting can become misleading if it focuses only on positive aspects or lacks sufficient context. Risks include overstating achievements, omitting limitations, or failing to explain boundaries and data gaps. These risks can lead to accusations of greenwashing or pinkwashing, which undermine trust.

## Credible ESG Communication

## Key factors for a credible ESG communication

Credible ESG communication starts with realistic and achievable goals. Organisations should clearly communicate their level of ambition while avoiding exaggerated claims. Messages should be adapted to different audiences, recognising that investors, employees, customers and communities have different expectations and concerns.

Transparency is essential. ESG communication should be regular, consistent and based on actual impacts rather than intentions alone. KPIs play a crucial role, as they provide evidence for claims and allow stakeholders to track progress over time. Digital tools and social media can help reach wider audiences, but they must be used responsibly and supported by reliable data.

## Implementing a credible ESG communication strategy into your company

## 1. Establish a common understanding of your company’s status quo to serve as a baseline

- As-is analysis of current capacity and corporate processes for disclosing sustainability information.
- Identify what relevant financial and non-financial regulations require in terms of sustainability reporting.
- Create a baseline technical approach for sustainability disclosures, including KPIs and metrics.
- Understand existing ESG commitments.
- Industry-specific benchmarking using relevant methodological criteria from ESG rating agencies.

## 2. Develop processes to ensure compliance with applicable ESG regulations

- Gaps can be identified from as-is analysis and then closed.
- Conduct workshop-based gap analysis to overcome knowledge deficits and create a common understanding.

## 3. Develop target operating model

- Take ESG ratings and public ESG commitments into account, analysing which ESG commitments are a good fit for your company.
- Consider sustainability disclosure operating model, overall strategic ambitions and data technology that you have available.
- Create appropriate IT structures and internal communication processes.

## 4. Draw implementation roadmap for ESG-related communication

Draw an implementation roadmap for ESG-related communication, integrating all three previous steps. This should be accompanied by a uniform reporting process.

## Opportunities for communicating your ESG performance

Organisations can strengthen their ESG credibility by participating in initiatives and commitments that align with their strategy. ESG ratings can also be used as communication tools, although it is recommended to consider results from at least two rating agencies, as methodologies differ.

You may consider joining additional initiatives and commitments in order to improve your ESG performance and reputation:

- Use ESG ratings for communication.
- Use scores from at least two different agencies to reliably assess your ESG performance, since the methodology for calculating ratings varies within different agencies.
- Prevent greenwashing and pinkwashing.

> **Greenwashing** means corporate misconduct in which a company’s own sustainability performance is exaggerated or misrepresented.
> **Pinkwashing** refers to exaggerating information about the social and governance performance.
