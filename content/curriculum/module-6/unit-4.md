# Unit 4: Continuous Improvement, Future EU Policies and Global ESG Trends

## Unit 4 – Introduction

ESG is a dynamic field shaped by evolving regulations, market expectations and societal challenges. Organisations that treat ESG as a one-off project risk falling behind.

This unit focuses on continuous improvement, helping learners understand how to anticipate future developments, adapt ESG strategies over time, and embed learning and reflection into long-term sustainability management.

## Future EU Policies

## Policies from the EU Commission

The EU Commission proposed amendment to European Climate Law to set an EU climate target for 2040 which will reaffirm the EU’s determination to tackle climate change and ensure path to climate neutrality by 2050.

Reducing emissions by 90% by 2040 will:

- ensure predictability for citizens, businesses and investors;
- boost the competitiveness of Europe’s businesses;
- enable EU to lead in developing clean technology markets of the future.

The Commission proposed series of changes as part of its February 2025 omnibus package:

- “Stop the clock” proposal: delays reporting requirements for companies currently in the scope of CSRD until 2028.
- Voluntary reporting standard for companies out of scope of CSRD: the Commission intends to issue a recommendation on voluntary reporting by SMEs which is based on EFRAG’s VSME standard.

## Steps for best implementation of future policies

The proposed target set by the European Commission establishes the tone for regulatory expectations in the years ahead and sends a clear signal to companies across all sectors.

It pushes organisations to reevaluate their carbon strategies, with particular attention to Scope 3 emissions, which include all indirect greenhouse gas emissions generated along a company’s value chain. This shift requires businesses to look beyond their direct operations and take responsibility for upstream and downstream impacts.

At the same time, companies are expected to embed net-zero roadmaps directly into their corporate governance structures, ensuring that climate objectives are integrated into strategic planning and decision-making processes at board level.

Aligning disclosures with EU regulations, such as the CSRD and ESRS frameworks, becomes essential to ensure transparency, comparability, and regulatory compliance. In parallel, organisations are encouraged to prioritise green innovation across operations and supply chains, fostering new sustainable products, processes, and partnerships that support long-term decarbonisation goals.

## Suggestions

In this evolving regulatory context, several strategic actions can support companies in strengthening their ESG positioning.

Conducting a double materiality assessment aligned with the CSRD and ESRS standards is a crucial first step, as it allows organisations to identify and prioritise both the impact of sustainability issues on the company and the company’s impact on society and the environment.

Mapping Scope 1, Scope 2, and especially Scope 3 emissions is equally important, particularly for companies operating in high-impact sectors. A comprehensive emissions inventory provides the foundation for credible transition strategies and measurable targets.

Furthermore, investing in training programmes for internal teams and board members on ESG topics and climate disclosure requirements ensures that sustainability considerations are embedded across governance and operational levels.

Finally, engaging third-party ESG assurance providers can enhance credibility, strengthen investor trust, and demonstrate a concrete commitment to transparency and accountability.

## Global ESG Trends

At a global level, institutional investors are largely maintaining their ESG commitments, although recent data reveals significant geographical differences. In the United States, for instance, the market has experienced its tenth consecutive quarter of ESG fund withdrawals, amounting to 6.1 billion dollars. These trends have been influenced in part by political developments, including the policy direction associated with President Donald Trump.

Despite this shift in the US context, commitment to ESG principles remains strong in Europe.

Europe’s regulatory landscape is currently undergoing a significant transformation. The European Commission has adopted the Omnibus I package, a set of broad simplification measures covering several legislative areas, including sustainable finance rules, the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), and investment frameworks.

Through this package, the Commission aims to reduce administrative burdens for companies while preserving the core objectives of the sustainability framework. The intention is to maintain ambition while improving clarity, coherence, and efficiency in implementation.

More broadly, ESG is increasingly being framed by forward-thinking companies, financial institutions, and investors not as a cost centre, but as a driver of resilience and competitive advantage.

Europe continues to lead the sustainable finance transition, with 83% of the world’s ESG assets managed by European investors. Investor commitment remains strong, and voluntary disclosure practices are expanding.

Financial institutions and corporations are progressively integrating sustainability into their core value creation strategies, recognising that long-term competitiveness and financial performance are closely linked to responsible environmental, social, and governance practices.

# Module Conclusion

Monitoring, reporting and adaptation are what make ESG strategies credible and effective in practice. Throughout this module, learners have explored how to measure ESG performance using relevant indicators, communicate progress transparently, leverage innovation, and stay prepared for future regulatory and market developments.

By applying these tools and approaches, organisations can move beyond symbolic commitments and ensure that ESG principles guide real decisions and behaviours over time. Continuous reflection, data-driven management and open communication are essential to maintaining trust and achieving lasting sustainability impact.
