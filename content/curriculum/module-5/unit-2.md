# Unit 2: ESG Data Gathering, Management, and Digitalisation

## Training Objectives

Upon completing the Unit, you should be able to:

- Recognise key types of ESG data.
- Apply practical methods to collect, manage, and digitalise ESG information effectively.

## Content

ESG data is the backbone of credible sustainability practice. It provides the facts behind your organisation’s environmental, social, and governance performance — the proof that words are turning into action. Without reliable data, ESG goals remain intentions rather than measurable progress.

Collecting, managing, and analysing ESG data is not only about compliance with regulations such as the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD). It’s also about making better decisions. When you know where resources are used inefficiently, which social programmes make a difference, or how governance measures improve trust, you can direct your efforts where they matter most.

## 1. Understanding ESG Data: What to Measure

Every organisation, regardless of size, generates valuable ESG data. To make sense of it, it helps to categorize information into three main areas — environmental, social, and governance.

- **Environmental data** describes how your activities affect natural resources. It may include energy consumption, water use, carbon emissions, waste volumes, or the share of renewable energy in your operations. Even small details — such as how often lights are left on after hours — can help you see where improvements are possible.
- **Social data** focuses on people — your employees, customers, and communities. It includes workplace safety, diversity and inclusion, training participation, employee satisfaction, and community engagement. For instance, tracking gender balance or employee turnover can highlight whether your organisation is creating an equitable and stable work environment.
- **Governance data** relates to transparency, ethics, and decision-making. It might include board composition, anti-corruption measures, data privacy incidents, or the number of employees completing compliance training. Governance data ensures accountability — the “G” that holds the other two pillars together.

These categories are interconnected. For example, energy reduction, which is environmental, can lower costs and improve working conditions, which are social, while strong internal policies, which are governance-related, ensure that reporting on both remains transparent.

## 2. Building a Data Strategy: Start Simple, Stay Consistent

The biggest challenge for many organisations is knowing where to begin. You don’t need expensive software or consultants to start gathering ESG data. What matters most is consistency — using the same indicators, in the same way, over time.

Begin with what’s already available. Utility bills, HR databases, or supplier invoices contain valuable insights. You might start by measuring energy use per month, diversity rates in hiring, or the number of internal ethics complaints.

Set a realistic timeline for data collection. Quarterly reviews work well because they show trends without overwhelming your team. Assign one responsible person per data category to ensure accountability.

Develop a data map — a simple overview showing where each data point comes from, who collects it, and how often. This prevents duplication and ensures everyone knows their role. Over time, your data map becomes a reference for the entire ESG reporting process.

## 3. Ensuring Data Quality and Credibility

Reliable data builds trust — both inside and outside the organisation. To maintain credibility, follow three golden rules: accuracy, comparability, and traceability.

- **Accuracy** means the data truly represents what is happening. If energy bills cover multiple sites, note that clearly. Don’t round numbers excessively or remove “uncomfortable” data — transparency is more valuable than perfection.
- **Comparability** allows you to track progress over time. Use consistent measurement units, for example kWh, liters, or CO₂ tons, and keep historical records.
- **Traceability** ensures that data sources can be verified. Save original files or screenshots so results can be checked if needed.

These principles also align with major frameworks such as GRI (Global Reporting Initiative) and SASB (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board), which emphasise data integrity and context. Following such standards now makes it much easier to adapt to upcoming EU regulations later.

## 4. The Role of Digitalisation

Digitalisation transforms ESG data management from manual tracking to dynamic insight. For SMEs, it doesn’t require complex systems — even simple tools can make a big difference.

You can start with spreadsheets organised by ESG theme, using formulas to calculate changes over time. As data volume grows, move to cloud-based dashboards such as Microsoft Power BI, Google Looker Studio, or specialised ESG software. These tools automate collection, visualize results, and make it easier to share reports with management or stakeholders.

Automation reduces human error and saves time. For instance, connecting your electricity supplier’s online portal directly to a dashboard can automatically update your energy data each month. HR systems can link employee data with social metrics like gender diversity or training hours.

Digitalisation also supports transparency. When ESG data is stored and visualized in real time, managers can respond faster to trends — such as rising emissions or declining employee satisfaction — and adjust actions accordingly.

## Example

A family-owned logistics company began tracking its vehicle fuel use manually. After digitalising the process with GPS-linked dashboards, it discovered idling accounted for 12% of fuel consumption. With simple driver training and route optimisation, they cut emissions by 10% and saved €8,000 annually.

Digitalisation turned scattered data into actionable insight.
