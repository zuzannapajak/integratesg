# Unit 2: Social Responsibility in Workforce and Supply Chain

## Training Objectives

Upon completing this unit, you should be able to:

- Assess social responsibility risks and improvement points in your workforce practices and supplier relationships.
- Implement simple actions that support fair working conditions, inclusion, and responsible supply chain engagement in daily operations.

## Start with people, not policies

Social responsibility becomes real in two places: how you treat people in your own business and how you work with suppliers. This is not only about formal HR systems or long supplier codes. In an SME, it usually starts with simple daily choices.

Start inside your own business. Pick 3–5 routine areas where people-related issues are most likely to appear. In many SMEs, these are:

- onboarding new staff
- shift planning
- task allocation
- safety checks
- complaint handling
- access to training or better assignments

Now do a short walk-through. Ask yourself:

- Where can someone get hurt, confused, or overloaded?
- Where do informal habits create unfairness?
- Where do people depend too much on one manager’s mood or memory?
- Where might someone feel excluded or unable to speak up?

Look for simple warning signs:

- repeated overtime for the same people
- unclear instructions that lead to mistakes
- new staff left without proper guidance
- training offered only informally
- safety basics skipped during busy periods
- people not knowing how to raise a concern

These are your first social responsibility hotspots.

## Build three simple workforce routines

You do not need a large HR system to improve social responsibility. You need a few routines that happen regularly.

## 1. Daily safety and clarity check

At the start of the day or shift, ask:

- Is anything unsafe today?
- Is anyone doing a new task without guidance?
- Is anything likely to create stress, confusion, or rushed work?

If you find one problem, fix one problem. That is enough for a start.

## 2. Weekly fairness check

Once a week, review three things:

- Who is overloaded?
- Who got access to better tasks, shifts, or learning opportunities?
- Where are mistakes happening because work is unclear or unevenly shared?

This helps you spot unfair patterns before they become “normal.”

## 3. Basic onboarding routine

Every new worker should get a short introduction covering:

- safety basics
- who to ask for help
- how breaks and work time are handled
- how to report a problem or near-miss
- what respectful behaviour looks like

This does not need to be long. It needs to be clear and repeatable.

## Make inclusion practical

Inclusion is often misunderstood as a separate project. In daily operations, it is mostly about removing unfair barriers.

Two simple habits help a lot:

- **Announce good opportunities to everyone** — If there is training, a better shift, a new responsibility, or a useful project, do not share it only through informal conversations.
- **Use one clear rule for flexibility** — For example, allow shift swaps with notice, publish schedules earlier, or offer a small flexible start window where possible.

These steps reduce favouritism and help people feel they are treated fairly. You do not need to solve every issue at once. But you should avoid a system where some people always hear about opportunities first and others are expected just to cope.

## Check social risks in your supply chain

A large part of your social impact may sit outside your company, in the suppliers you use. That is why social responsibility also includes supply chain responsibility.

Do not try to screen every supplier at once. Start with your top 5–10 suppliers based on one of these factors:

- they cost you the most
- they affect service quality or safety
- they create reputational risk
- they are important for continuity of supply

Then apply a simple minimum check. Ask whether the supplier can confirm:

- basic safe working conditions
- legal employment practices
- respect for fair treatment
- clear responsibility for complaints and problems

Then place the supplier into one of three groups:

- **Keep** — they meet your minimum standard
- **Improve** — they have gaps but are willing to improve
- **Replace later** — they avoid transparency or reject basic expectations

This approach is better than demanding perfection. In real business, you often need suppliers to improve step by step, not disappear overnight. You do not need a full audit. A short written answer, a policy they already use, or a clear explanation of routine practice is enough for an SME-level first step.

## Turn expectations into action

For both workforce and suppliers, social responsibility works best when expectations are simple and visible.

Inside your business, this could mean:

- one-page onboarding checklist
- weekly workload review
- visible rule for sharing training opportunities
- one speak-up channel for concerns

With suppliers, this could mean:

- one short message explaining your minimum expectations
- one improvement request within 90 days
- one follow-up check during the next review or quote process

A small company does not need to present itself as perfect. It needs to show that it handles people and partners responsibly in normal work.

For example:

- A business reduces staff frustration by publishing schedules earlier and using a clearer shift-swap rule.
- A warehouse improves safety by giving every new worker a short onboarding routine on day one.
- A company keeps an important supplier but asks for one clear improvement in safety practice and checks progress after three months.

These are strong moves because they improve trust without creating heavy bureaucracy.
