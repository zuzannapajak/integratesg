# Unit 4: Credible ESG in Customer Communication and Claims

In business operations, ESG becomes visible when it affects the customer experience. This includes packaging, delivery, repair, complaints, product use, service quality, and communication. If you say your business is responsible, but the customer cannot see any real change, your message will quickly lose credibility.

## Training Objectives

Upon completing this unit, you should be able to:

- Evaluate customer-facing ESG claims and operational changes to identify greenwashing or social washing risks.
- Apply practical rules for communicating ESG actions in a specific, credible, and proportionate way that reflects real business operations.

## Recognise the warning signs of weak ESG communication

A claim becomes risky when it sounds impressive but says very little. You should be careful if your message uses words like: green, eco-friendly, ethical, sustainable, responsible, people-first. These words are not wrong on their own. The problem starts when they are not supported by clear facts.

Typical warning signs include:

- the claim is broad, but the change is very small
- the message describes intentions, not actions
- the claim cannot be checked or explained
- the business talks about values, but daily operations stay the same
- customer communication is strong, but internal practice is weak

For example, saying “we care about the planet” is weak. Saying “we reduced packaging size for our top three products to cut material use” is much stronger.

## Separate operational improvement from marketing language

A useful test is this: first describe the operational change, and only then decide whether any customer-facing message is needed.

Start with questions like these:

- What exactly changed in the process?
- Where did the change happen?
- What problem was it meant to solve?
- What result can you already see?
- Is the change large enough to mention externally?

This helps you separate real improvement from pure promotion.

A real operational improvement might be:

- switching to packaging with recycled content
- reducing delivery trips through better scheduling
- improving repair instead of replacement
- using simpler, more accessible customer information
- reducing unnecessary paper in customer communication

A pure marketing move might be:

- adding a green slogan to your website
- changing label colour to look more natural
- highlighting one tiny action as if it transformed the whole business

The rule is simple: if the process has not changed, the claim should not get stronger.

## Design customer-visible ESG improvements carefully

When you improve ESG in customer-facing operations, choose changes that customers can understand and that fit real business needs.

A good starting point is to map one key customer journey:

- **before purchase** — quote, information, order process
- **delivery or service** — packaging, travel, punctuality, safety, communication
- **after-sales** — complaints, returns, repairs, follow-up

Then ask where the customer can see one of these issues:

- too much waste
- unclear communication
- poor treatment
- avoidable delays
- unnecessary repeat visits
- weak complaint handling
- inaccessible or confusing information

Now choose one or two visible improvements. Good examples include:

- reducing packaging volume without increasing damage
- replacing printed documents with simpler digital communication
- making complaint handling faster and more transparent
- giving clearer delivery windows to reduce missed visits

These actions are credible because they are linked to actual operations, not only to image.

## Communicate only what is specific, checkable, and proportionate

Before making any ESG claim, use this simple filter. Ask whether the message is:

- **Specific** — does it describe a real action?
- **Checkable** — could you explain or show what changed?
- **Proportionate** — does the wording match the scale of the action?

This matters because even a good action can become weak communication if the language is exaggerated.

Compare these examples:

| Weak                                                 | Better                                                                                           |
| ---------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| We are a sustainable company                         | We introduced recycled-content packaging for selected products.                                  |
| We care deeply about people.                         | We introduced a clearer complaints process and committed to responding within five working days. |
| Our service is ethical and environmentally friendly. | We reduced repeat delivery trips by using tighter scheduling and clearer customer updates.       |

The second versions work better because they describe what changed, not just how the company wants to be seen.

## Evaluate trade-offs before you communicate

Not every ESG improvement is automatically the best option. In real operations, you often need to balance cost, quality, time, ESG outcome, and customer trust.

Use a simple decision card with five questions:

- **Cost** — does this increase or reduce cost?
- **Quality** — does it affect product or service performance?
- **Time** — does it make work slower or faster?
- **ESG outcome** — what exactly improves?
- **Customer trust** — will the customer experience become clearer, fairer, or more reliable?

For example, a new packaging option may cost slightly more, but reduce waste and improve customer perception. That may still be a good decision if damage rates stay stable.

Another example: tighter appointment windows may require better planning, but they reduce missed visits and customer frustration. That improves trust, even if the internal process becomes a bit stricter.

## Build credibility through small, documented changes

Credibility usually does not come from one big campaign. It comes from small improvements that are real, repeated, and documented.

You can build this step by step:

- choose one visible improvement
- test it for a short period
- check whether quality stays acceptable
- record what changed
- communicate the result in simple language

For example:

> We reduced packaging size for our most common orders. We tested the change for two weeks. Damage rates did not increase. We now use the new standard as part of our normal packing routine.

This kind of message is strong because it shows restraint. It does not try to claim too much. It shows that the business made a practical decision and can stand behind it.
