# Unit 1: Key Frameworks – SDGs, GRI, SASB, TCFD, ESRS

## Training objectives

Upon completing this unit, you should be able to:

- Differentiate between the main ESG frameworks and describe their key purposes and focus areas.
- Select and apply the framework or combination of frameworks most relevant to your organisation’s goals, size, and sector.
- Follow the basic steps to align your organisation’s sustainability reporting with recognised international standards.

## Introduction

When you first encounter ESG reporting, the number of frameworks can feel overwhelming. SDGs, GRI, SASB, TCFD, ESRS – each brings its own logic and audience. The good news is that these frameworks complement rather than compete with each other. They help you tell a transparent and credible story about your organisation’s impact on the environment, society, and governance – and how these factors influence your business success.

## Understanding the Landscape

Think of ESG reporting frameworks as different “lenses” through which you can view your organisation’s performance.

- The SDGs give you the global goals to aim for.
- The GRI provides a comprehensive reporting structure for your impacts on people and planet.
- The SASB zooms in on financially material issues for investors.
- The TCFD focuses specifically on climate-related risks and opportunities.
- The ESRS sets the mandatory European baseline for sustainability disclosures under the EU’s Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD).

If you understand the purpose of each, you can build a reporting approach that fits your business rather than forcing your business into someone else’s model.

## The Main Frameworks Explained

## Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

Developed by the United Nations, the 17 SDGs act as the universal language of sustainability. They help you connect your company’s activities with wider global challenges such as poverty, gender equality, and climate action. For example, if your business invests in energy-efficient machinery, you are contributing to SDG 7 – Affordable and Clean Energy.

## How to use them

- Identify which SDGs link naturally to your business operations or products.
- Prioritise 3–5 goals that you can influence directly.
- Integrate them into your strategy and use them as a communication tool when talking about impact.

## Global Reporting Initiative (GRI)

GRI Standards are the world’s most widely adopted sustainability reporting framework. They help you describe how your organisation affects the economy, the environment and society. Unlike financial reporting, which focuses on shareholders, GRI is stakeholder-centred – addressing anyone affected by your activities.

## How to use it

- Conduct a materiality assessment – identify which ESG issues matter most to your stakeholders.
- Gather data for the relevant GRI indicators, for example energy use, diversity or community engagement.
- Prepare your report following GRI structure and ensure it is consistent year by year.
- Communicate your findings clearly – not just as numbers, but as stories that illustrate change.

For instance, a training centre might use GRI to report on how it reduces energy use (GRI 302) or promotes gender equality in staffing (GRI 405).

## Sustainability Accounting Standards Board (SASB)

SASB focuses on financial materiality. It asks: “Which ESG issues could influence our financial performance?” It offers specific standards for 77 industries.

## How to use it

- Identify your industry category in the SASB database.
- Review the suggested ESG topics, for example data security, product quality or employee health.
- Collect data using the recommended metrics.
- Integrate results into your financial or management reports.

If you run a manufacturing SME, SASB helps you identify the ESG issues that most directly affect profitability, such as energy efficiency or waste management.

## Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD)

TCFD is all about climate risks and resilience. It guides you to disclose how climate change might affect your business model, investments, and strategy.

## How to use it

- Assess how climate risks, like flooding, regulation, or energy costs, could affect your operations.
- Create governance structures – who in your company oversees climate issues?
- Define clear metrics and targets, for example CO₂ reduction per product or energy source diversification.
- Report transparently to investors and partners.
- A small logistics company, for example, might use TCFD to evaluate how rising fuel prices or carbon taxes will affect delivery costs and to plan accordingly.

## European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS)

The ESRS were developed by EFRAG as part of the EU’s Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD). These are not optional – they are the legal reporting standards for companies within or connected to the EU market.

Their unique feature is double materiality: you must report both how sustainability issues affect your company and how your company affects society and the environment.

## How to use it

- Review the ESRS topics, environmental, social, and governance.
- Conduct a double materiality assessment.
- Align your existing GRI or TCFD reports with ESRS requirements.
- Prepare structured disclosures in line with CSRD deadlines.

Even if you’re a small company not yet legally obliged to report, familiarising yourself with ESRS now will make future compliance easier.

## Putting It All Together

To make these frameworks work for you:

- **Start small.** Choose one or two frameworks most relevant to your size and sector.
- **Combine wisely.** Many organisations use GRI for broad reporting, SASB for financial focus, and TCFD for climate risk.
- **Align with ESRS.** If you operate in the EU, use ESRS as your “north star” and check that your data and reporting meet its structure.
- **Keep it consistent.** Report regularly – annually if possible – and update your data collection systems.
- **Engage your team.** Sustainability reporting is not just a compliance task. It’s an opportunity to engage employees and show customers and partners that you act responsibly.

## A Practical Example

Imagine you manage a medium-sized textile company. You begin by mapping your work against the SDGs and identify three relevant ones: Responsible Consumption and Production (SDG 12), Decent Work and Economic Growth (SDG 8), and Climate Action (SDG 13).

You then use GRI indicators to measure water use and worker wellbeing. To satisfy investor interest, you adopt SASB’s metrics for textile manufacturing. Next, you assess your exposure to rising energy prices using TCFD and include that in your annual report. Finally, you ensure your disclosures align with ESRS, preparing for the upcoming EU requirements.

By combining these frameworks, your report becomes both globally relevant and locally compliant.

## Key Takeaway

Each framework offers a different piece of the ESG puzzle. You don’t need to master them all at once, but understanding their logic will help you choose the right approach for your organisation. Start with the ones that fit your priorities, keep your data accurate, and focus on progress, not perfection.

Once you’ve built the foundation, you’ll be ready to explore how EU regulation – especially the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) – shapes the reporting requirements further, which we’ll cover in the next unit.
