# Unit 2: The EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and related EU Policies

## Training objectives

Upon completing this unit, you should be able to:

- Explain the main purpose, scope, and requirements of the CSRD.
- Identify how the CSRD connects to other EU sustainability policies and frameworks.
- Apply the key steps your organisation needs to take to prepare for CSRD-compliant reporting.

## Introduction

If you work in or with a European business, you’ve probably noticed that sustainability reporting is no longer optional. The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) is the EU’s way of setting a common standard for how companies disclose their environmental, social, and governance (ESG) impacts.

At its core, the CSRD aims to make sustainability reporting as reliable and comparable as financial reporting. It gives investors, regulators, and the public a clear picture of how companies affect – and are affected by – sustainability issues.

## What Is the CSRD?

The CSRD replaces and expands the older Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD). It significantly increases both the number of companies required to report and the depth of information they must provide.

Under the CSRD, companies must disclose information according to the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) developed by EFRAG. These standards define what to report, how to report it, and to whom.

The CSRD applies gradually, depending on company size and listing status. Even if your organisation is not yet directly covered, the shift in market expectations means you will soon be affected – especially if you are part of a supply chain for larger companies that are required to report.

## The Principle of Double Materiality

One of the CSRD’s most important concepts is double materiality.

- **Impact materiality** looks at how your company’s activities affect society and the environment.
- **Financial materiality** looks at how sustainability issues affect your company’s financial performance.

To comply, you must assess both. For example, if you manufacture packaging, you report not only on the environmental impact of your materials, impact materiality, but also on how upcoming plastic taxes or recycling rules could influence your costs and competitiveness, financial materiality.

This approach ensures that your sustainability report gives a full picture – not just of your footprint, but also of the risks and opportunities you face.

## Key Steps to Prepare for CSRD-aligned Reporting

To prepare for CSRD-aligned reporting, you’ll need to follow a few key steps:

- **Understand your obligations.** Check if and when your company will be required to report under CSRD.
- **Conduct a double materiality assessment.** Identify and prioritise ESG topics that matter most to your business and stakeholders.
- **Gather relevant data.** Collect qualitative and quantitative information aligned with ESRS topics, for example energy use, gender equality, supply chain due diligence.
- **Integrate reporting into governance.** Assign clear responsibilities within your team – sustainability reporting should be part of your regular management cycle.
- **Engage your value chain.** Even small suppliers may need to provide data for customers’ CSRD reports.
- **Publish and verify.** Reports must be included in the management report, digitally tagged, and externally assured by an independent auditor.

The process can seem demanding at first, but breaking it down into manageable steps makes it achievable.

## Why it matters

Even if you’re not legally required to report yet, the CSRD affects you in several ways:

- **Market access:** Larger companies will expect CSRD-compliant data from their suppliers.
- **Financing:** Banks and investors increasingly ask for sustainability information to meet their own obligations under the EU Taxonomy and SFDR.
- **Reputation:** Transparent reporting strengthens trust with customers, employees, and communities.
- **Efficiency:** Tracking ESG data often reveals areas where you can save resources and costs.

In short, the CSRD sets the standard for responsible and future-proof business in Europe.

## How CSRD connects with other EU policies

The CSRD doesn’t stand alone – it’s part of the EU’s broader push for a sustainable finance ecosystem. The main related policies include:

- **EU Taxonomy Regulation:** Defines what counts as an “environmentally sustainable” activity. CSRD reports must disclose how much of a company’s activities align with these criteria.
- **Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR):** Requires investors and financial institutions to disclose sustainability risks and impacts, using CSRD data from companies.
- **European Green Deal:** Sets the political agenda for making Europe the first climate-neutral continent by 2050. The CSRD is a key tool for tracking progress.
- **Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD):** Requires companies to identify and address human rights and environmental risks throughout their value chains.

Together, these frameworks create a coherent system: the CSRD provides the data, the Taxonomy defines sustainability, the SFDR and CSDDD ensure transparency and accountability, and the Green Deal sets the overall direction.

## Example

Imagine you manage a medium-sized supplier in the automotive sector. Your main clients are large manufacturers already preparing CSRD-compliant reports. They now ask you for data on your energy consumption, labour practices, and waste management.

You start by mapping what information you already have – your electricity bills, staff training logs, and waste collection records. You then align this data with ESRS requirements, focusing on material topics such as energy efficiency (E1) and workforce wellbeing (S1).

By structuring your data collection and reporting early, you not only support your clients but also gain valuable insights to reduce costs and improve sustainability performance. When the CSRD eventually applies to your company, you’ll already have a strong foundation.

## Key takeaway

The CSRD is more than a compliance exercise. It’s a tool to help you understand and communicate your organisation’s real impact and resilience.

By starting early – understanding your material issues, gathering reliable data, and aligning with ESRS – you prepare your business for future requirements and position yourself as a credible partner in sustainable value chains.
