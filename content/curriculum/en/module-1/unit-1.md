# Unit 1: The Three Pillars of ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance)

## Training Objective

- Define the three core components of ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance).

## Content

Welcome! You're about to dive into the most fundamental question in sustainable business today: What exactly is ESG?

Think of ESG as a scorecard that helps investors, customers, and employees judge how a company manages its risks and opportunities outside of just financial performance. It stands for Environmental, Social, and Governance. If you can understand and apply these three pillars, you've taken the first major step toward integrating sustainability into your business.

Let’s break down each pillar so you can clearly define it and understand why it matters to your organisation.

## What Does ESG Actually Stand For?

ESG is not just a buzzword. It's an approach that integrates non-financial factors into business strategy to create value. The three letters represent three broad reporting or thematic areas.

## 1. The E for Environmental: Planet Protection

The Environmental pillar looks at a company's impact on the natural environment. Essentially, it covers what your business does to the planet — and what the planet does to your business, like climate risks.

As an SME manager or VET provider, you don't need to save the entire world, but you do need to manage your operation's footprint.

- **Climate Change and Carbon Footprint:** This is about tracking the greenhouse gases, like carbon dioxide, your business releases, from the energy you use to the transportation of your goods. Managing your carbon footprint is a key task.
- **Resource Efficiency:** This focuses on how efficiently you use resources. Are you wasting water or electricity? Are your processes designed to be energy-efficient?
- **Waste and Pollution:** This covers everything from managing manufacturing waste to reducing plastic in your office. The goal is to avoid causing pollution and contribute to a circular economy.

> **Circular Economy:** A model of production and consumption where materials are kept in use for as long as possible, aiming to eliminate waste.

## 2. The S for Social: People and Relationships

The Social pillar focuses on how a company treats people — its employees, customers, suppliers, and the communities it operates in. This is about your relationships and your reputation.

- **Employee Wellbeing and Safety:** This includes health, safety, job satisfaction, and ensuring your team feels respected. Employee wellbeing is a huge factor in talent retention.
- **Diversity, Equality, and Inclusion (DEI):** This involves ensuring your workplace is fair and accessible to people from all backgrounds, which is aligned with the project’s Equality, Diversity and Inclusion (EDI) principles. This means actively working to reduce barriers for underrepresented groups.
- **Supply Chain Responsibility:** Do you know if the businesses you buy from are treating their workers fairly? This is a key part of your social responsibility.
- **Community Engagement:** How does your business support the local community? This could be through local hiring or supporting a local charity.

## Reflection Question

- Take a moment to consider your current team. Are your internal policies explicitly designed to promote diversity and reduce barriers to employment?

## 3. The G for Governance: Leadership and Trust

The Governance pillar is about the internal engine of the company. It covers the rules, controls, and leadership structures that keep your business running ethically and legally. Good governance ensures that your E and S commitments are actually followed through.

- **Corporate Governance:** This covers who is in charge, including the board and management, and how decisions are made. Transparency in decision-making is critical.
- **Transparency and Accountability:** You need clear systems in place so that everyone knows what is expected of them, and leaders can be held accountable for their decisions.
- **Anti-Corruption:** This includes having strong policies against bribery, fraud, and other unethical behaviours.

## Why Governance Connects E and S

Think of Governance as the foundation. Without strong, transparent governance, any environmental or social commitments are likely to fail. Integrity in decision-making is what gives your ESG efforts credibility.

By mastering these three pillars — Environmental, Social, and Governance — you will have the clear, foundational knowledge needed to begin integrating ESG into your business processes.
