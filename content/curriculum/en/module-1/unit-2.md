# Unit 2: From Corporate Philanthropy to Business Necessity

## Training Objective

- Describe the historical evolution of ESG in the business landscape.

## Content

You just learned what ESG is today. Now, let’s look back at its journey. ESG wasn't invented overnight. It evolved over decades from something nice companies occasionally did to a non-negotiable part of business strategy. Understanding this history helps you explain why it matters so much now.

Think of the history of ESG as moving through three main stages: Exclusion, Inclusion, and Full Integration.

## 1. The Exclusion Era: Early Socially Responsible Investing (SRI)

Decades ago, the focus wasn't on what companies should do, but what investors didn't want to own. This was the birth of Socially Responsible Investing (SRI).

- **What it looked like:** Investors, often driven by ethical or religious beliefs, would simply exclude companies from their portfolios that were involved in specific sectors, such as tobacco, alcohol, or weapons.
- **The Big Idea:** This approach was about values alignment — making sure money wasn't supporting industries the investor morally opposed.
- **The Limit:** SRI focused on avoiding harm but didn't actually push companies to do better. It was passive, not active.

## 2. The Inclusion Era: The Rise of Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

By the late 20th century, the focus shifted from avoiding the bad to actively promoting the good. This is where Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) took centre stage.

- **What it looked like:** Companies started dedicating resources to social projects, charitable donations, and environmental initiatives. You probably remember seeing reports on a company's community day or its tree-planting efforts.
- **The Key Distinction:** CSR was largely seen as philanthropy or a separate add-on. It was often managed by a dedicated CSR department, separate from the core business strategy and financial decision-making.

> **Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR):** A business model that helps a company be socially accountable to itself, its stakeholders, and the public. It is generally voluntary and separate from core operations.

## Why CSR Wasn't Enough

While good-hearted, CSR often had two problems:

1. **It was often optional:** If profits dropped, CSR budgets were the first to be cut.
2. **It wasn't always linked to the business:** A factory dumping pollution but donating to a children's hospital, a practice sometimes called "greenwashing", wasn't truly sustainable.

This is what led to the demand for a more structured, financially relevant framework.

## 3. The Integration Era: The Birth of ESG

The term ESG gained real traction in the early 2000s, driven by global bodies and the investment community. They realised that environmental and social issues were actually financial risks and opportunities.

- **The Key Shift:** ESG moved the conversation from "being nice" (CSR) to "being smart" (ESG).
- **The Driving Force:** Investors started demanding information on E, S, and G factors because they understood these factors impact a company's long-term value, credit rating, and stability. Poor governance, for example, can lead to fraud or scandal, which destroys investor trust and money.
- **Global Frameworks:** Key global initiatives like the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and new EU legislation, like the CSRD, started pushing ESG from a voluntary best practice into a regulatory requirement.

Simply put: ESG is the business world’s response to the need for sustainable development.

## The Current Landscape: ESG as a Business Process

Today, ESG is not an add-on; it is an integrated business process.

- You, as a business manager or VET provider, are expected to treat ESG like any other operational factor, such as quality control or financial reporting.
- This approach aims to achieve a clear understanding of the meaning, value, and challenges of integrating ESG frameworks.

## Reflection Question

- Can you identify a past decision in your company that was purely CSR, meaning philanthropic, versus one that was ESG, meaning integrated risk management? How did the outcomes differ?
