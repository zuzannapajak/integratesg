# Unit 3: ESG Ratings, Audits, Disclosures and Their Impact

## Training objectives

Upon completing this unit, you should be able to:

- Explain how ESG ratings, audits, and disclosures work and why they matter for organisations of all sizes.
- Evaluate how ESG performance influences investor confidence, reputation, and long-term competitiveness.
- Apply practical steps to prepare your organisation for ESG evaluation and external assurance.

## Introduction

After understanding ESG frameworks and the CSRD, the next question is: How do others know whether your organisation is truly performing well on ESG issues?

This is where ESG ratings, audits, and disclosures come in. They turn your sustainability commitments into measurable, verifiable results. Together, they form the bridge between what you say and what you can prove about your ESG performance.

## What Are ESG Ratings?

ESG ratings are assessments of how well your organisation manages environmental, social, and governance risks and opportunities. They are usually produced by independent rating agencies, research firms, or financial institutions.

Each agency uses its own methodology, but most evaluate similar factors such as:

- **Environmental:** carbon emissions, resource efficiency, pollution management.
- **Social:** labour conditions, diversity, community impact.
- **Governance:** board structure, ethics, transparency.

You can think of ESG ratings like a credit score for sustainability. A high rating tells investors and customers that your organisation is well-managed, forward-thinking, and lower-risk. A low rating suggests weaknesses or insufficient data transparency.

Major rating agencies include MSCI, Sustainalytics, S&P Global, and Moody’s ESG Solutions.

## What Are ESG Audits and Disclosures?

While ratings are external assessments, ESG audits are more like an internal or third-party “health check”. They examine your ESG data, controls, and reporting processes to ensure accuracy and credibility.

An ESG disclosure, on the other hand, is the information you publish about your sustainability performance – typically through an annual report or your website.

Together:

- **Disclosures** are what you report.
- **Audits** confirm that what you report is accurate.
- **Ratings** interpret how strong your performance appears compared to others.

Under the CSRD, ESG disclosures must now follow the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) and will be externally assured, meaning auditors will verify that the data is correct.

## Why They Matter

In today’s market, ESG ratings and verified disclosures directly influence:

- **Access to finance:** Banks and investors use ESG scores to assess risk and lending terms. A stronger ESG profile can mean lower borrowing costs.
- **Business partnerships:** Large corporations prefer suppliers with credible sustainability data and certifications.
- **Brand and reputation:** Transparent reporting builds trust with employees, customers, and communities.
- **Regulatory compliance:** Verified disclosures help you meet CSRD and other legal requirements.

Even for SMEs, these benefits are real. A clear ESG disclosure can set you apart from competitors and open new funding opportunities.

## How ESG Ratings Are Calculated

Each rating agency uses its own scoring model, but the process generally includes:

- Collecting publicly available data from reports, websites, and regulatory filings.
- Analysing policies, targets, and performance indicators.
- Comparing results with peers in the same industry.
- Adjusting for controversies, fines, or incidents.

It’s important to remember that transparency is key. If data is missing, outdated, or inconsistent, it can hurt your score – even if your actual performance is good.

## How to Prepare Your Organisation

You don’t need to be a large corporation to manage your ESG evaluation effectively. Start with these simple steps:

- **Map your ESG data.** Identify what you already track – such as energy use, staff turnover, or supplier audits – and where the gaps are.
- **Align with recognised frameworks.** Use GRI or ESRS indicators to ensure your data is comparable.
- **Set measurable targets.** For example, reducing energy consumption by 10% in a year or achieving gender balance in management roles.
- **Document your evidence.** Keep records of data sources, policies, and actions taken.
- **Engage an independent reviewer.** A light external audit or validation adds credibility even before CSRD assurance becomes mandatory.
- **Monitor progress regularly.** Update your disclosures annually to show continuous improvement.

By following these steps, you demonstrate accountability and make future audits or ratings much easier.

## Example

Imagine you run a small manufacturing business that recently introduced solar panels and improved workplace safety training. You decide to publish a short ESG disclosure on your website, following GRI indicators for energy (GRI 302) and occupational health (GRI 403).

An external consultant reviews your data to ensure accuracy – a form of light ESG audit. Later, a rating agency uses your publicly available data to assign an ESG score. Because your disclosure is clear, complete, and consistent, your business receives a strong rating.

As a result, a potential investor sees your rating and contacts you for collaboration. Transparency pays off.

## Common Challenges

When starting with ESG ratings and disclosures, many SMEs face similar obstacles:

- Limited time and expertise to collect and verify data.
- Uncertainty about which indicators are most relevant.
- Confusion about differing rating methodologies.

The best solution is to start small and stay consistent. Choose a few key indicators, publish them clearly, and build from there. Over time, your credibility and data quality will grow together.

## Key Takeaway

ESG ratings, audits, and disclosures are essential tools for turning sustainability into measurable business value. They help you build trust, attract investment, and strengthen competitiveness.

Even simple, transparent reporting is better than silence. Begin by aligning your disclosures with recognised standards, keeping your data reliable, and engaging external assurance when possible.
