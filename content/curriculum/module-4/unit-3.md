# Unit 3: Governance in Operational Decision-Making

Governance can sound like something meant for boards, reports, or large corporations. In daily business operations, it is much simpler than that. It is about how decisions are made, who is responsible, what gets approved, and what happens when something goes wrong.

## Training Objectives

Upon completing this unit, you should be able to:

- Analyse everyday business decisions to define clear roles, approvals, and escalation paths that improve transparency and accountability.
- Implement simple governance controls that support ethical behaviour, reduce corruption risk, and make concerns easier to raise and resolve.

## Map the decisions that matter most

Start by listing the decisions that happen often or carry risk. Do not map everything. Focus on the decisions that affect money, people, suppliers, complaints, or trust.

A practical list might include:

- purchases above a set amount
- supplier selection or supplier change
- customer complaints and refunds
- discounts or exceptions
- hiring or role changes
- safety incidents
- data access
- approval of expenses or invoices

Now create a simple table with three columns:

- Decision
- Who decides
- How it is approved

Keep one rule from the start: assign responsibility to a role, not to a person. For example, write “Operations Lead” or “Owner,” not an employee’s name.

This map gives you two immediate benefits:

- people know who owns the decision
- you reduce confusion, delay, and blame-shifting

## Build a simple approvals and escalation map

Once you know who decides, define what happens when the normal path is not enough. This is your approval and escalation path.

You do not need a flowchart for every case. Start with 3–5 common situations where escalation matters:

- a purchase is above the normal limit
- a supplier fails a basic requirement
- a customer complaint may lead to refund or legal risk
- a safety issue is repeated
- someone reports unethical behaviour

For each one, define:

- What triggers escalation
- Who receives it first
- Who becomes the next level if it is not resolved
- How fast it should be handled

Example:

- **Trigger:** purchase above agreed threshold
- **First level:** team lead
- **Next level:** operations manager or owner
- **Timeline:** within 2 working days

This keeps decisions visible and prevents the common SME problem where difficult cases stay stuck because nobody is sure who should act.

## Introduce a micro code of ethics

A code of ethics does not need to be long. In fact, a short one works better in daily operations. The current module draft already points toward a micro code of ethics as a practical governance tool.

Keep yours short and operational. It can be 6–8 lines. For example:

- We keep records honest.
- We do not accept bribes, gifts, or favours that influence decisions.
- We treat customers, colleagues, and suppliers fairly.
- We raise concerns when something feels wrong.
- We protect personal and business data.
- We follow approval rules, even under time pressure.

This kind of code helps because it translates governance into everyday behaviour. It also supports ethical leadership. People copy what leaders tolerate, ignore, or reward. If shortcuts are normal, ethics disappear fast. If leaders follow the same rules they expect from others, governance becomes credible.

## Apply anti-corruption minimum rules

Many SMEs think corruption controls are only for large companies. In practice, every small business needs a minimum standard.

You can start with five simple rules:

- No personal gifts or favours linked to supplier or purchasing decisions
- No verbal-only approval for important spending
- No invoice payment without a basic check
- No supplier selection without at least one clear reason recorded
- No private side deals that bypass company rules

These are small controls, but they matter. They reduce the risk of hidden pressure, personal benefit, and poor decisions dressed up as urgency.

A useful example is purchasing. If one employee can choose a supplier, approve the order, and confirm the invoice alone, the risk is obvious. A better approach is simple separation:

- one role requests
- one role approves
- one role records or checks

Even in a very small firm, this logic can often be applied at a basic level.

## Make transparency visible in daily operations

Transparency does not mean publishing everything. It means making important decisions clear, traceable, and understandable.

Start with four areas:

- **Purchases** — record who approved, why, and at what limit
- **Complaints** — record what happened, who reviewed it, and what decision was made
- **Supplier changes** — note why the supplier was chosen, kept, or replaced
- **Concerns or incidents** — log what was reported and how it was handled

You do not need special software. A shared spreadsheet, form, or notebook can be enough if it is used consistently. This is where governance becomes operational. You are not writing theory. You are making decisions easier to explain and harder to hide.

## Set up a simple speak-up channel

People need a safe way to raise concerns. The existing material already treats a speak-up or whistleblowing channel as a practical governance tool and frames it as a lightweight, trustworthy process for SMEs.

Keep it simple. Define:

- one reporting channel — for example, one email address or one named contact role
- one backup receiver — in case the first person is involved
- one response promise — for example, reply within 7 days
- one protection rule — no retaliation for good-faith reporting

Then define the mini process:

- receive the message
- confirm receipt
- review the facts confidentially
- decide the next step
- close the case or explain what happens next

This matters because problems grow in silence. A speak-up channel is not only for serious scandals. It also helps with smaller issues before they become larger operational or reputational risks.
