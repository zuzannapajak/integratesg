# Unit 2: Setting ESG priorities and objectives

## Training Objectives

Upon completing Unit 2, you will be able to:

- Prioritise ESG issues relevant to your organisation by evaluating their significance to stakeholders and business strategy.
- Formulate clear, measurable ESG objectives that align with organisational priorities and support long-term value creation.

## Content

## Introduction: Why Prioritising ESG Matters

Imagine you’re at a buffet with dozens of dishes. You can’t eat everything, right? The same goes for ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) issues in your organisation. You need to choose what matters most — otherwise, you’ll spread yourself too thin and make little impact. This unit will help you figure out how to pick your ESG “dishes” wisely and set clear goals for real progress.

Prioritising ESG isn’t just about compliance or ticking boxes. It’s about making a real difference for your organisation, your stakeholders, and the world. By the end of this unit, you’ll know how to identify what matters most, set meaningful objectives, and communicate your progress with confidence.

## Section 1: What Does It Mean to Prioritise ESG?

> Materiality is the principle of determining which ESG issues are significant enough to influence the decisions of stakeholders and the long-term success of the organisation.

Not every ESG topic will be equally important for your business. Prioritising means focusing on the issues that matter most to your stakeholders and your strategy. This is the foundation for effective ESG management and reporting.

## Why Prioritisation is Essential

- **Resource allocation:** You have limited time, money, and people. Prioritisation ensures you invest where it counts.
- **Stakeholder trust:** Focusing on what matters most builds credibility with investors, customers, employees, and regulators.
- **Long-term value:** Addressing material ESG issues supports sustainable growth and resilience.

## Reflection question

What ESG topics do you think your stakeholders care about most? Why?

## Section 2: Steps to Prioritise ESG Issues

## Step 1: Identify Relevant ESG Topics

Start by making a list of all possible ESG topics that could affect your organisation. These might include:

- Climate change and carbon emissions
- Water use and waste
- Employee wellbeing and diversity
- Supply chain ethics
- Data privacy
- Community engagement
- Board diversity and governance
- Human rights
- Product safety
- Anti-corruption

**Tip:** Check out frameworks like GRI (Global Reporting Initiative), SASB (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board), or the UN Sustainable Development Goals for inspiration. These resources provide comprehensive lists of ESG topics relevant to different industries.

## Step 2: Engage Your Stakeholders

You can’t set priorities alone. Ask your employees, customers, investors, suppliers, and community members:

- Which ESG issues matter most to you?
- Where do you see the biggest risks or opportunities?
- How do our current practices impact you or your community?

## Real-life example

A European retailer surveyed its customers and discovered that ethical sourcing was a top concern. As a result, they made responsible sourcing a key ESG priority. Similarly, a tech company found that data privacy was a top issue for its users, leading to new privacy initiatives.

## Step 3: Assess Business Impact

For each ESG issue, ask yourself:

- How does this issue affect our business performance, reputation, or compliance?
- What are the risks if we ignore it?
- What opportunities could we unlock by addressing it?
- Are there regulatory requirements or industry standards we must meet?

## Reflection question

Which ESG issue, if left unaddressed, could most harm your organisation’s reputation or bottom line?

## Step 4: Create a Materiality Matrix

A materiality matrix helps you visualize which ESG issues are most important to both stakeholders and your business.

Here’s how:

- Draw a simple grid.
- X-axis: Importance to stakeholders.
- Y-axis: Impact on business.
- Plot each issue.
- The ones in the top right corner, high on both axes, are your top priorities.

A manufacturing company used a materiality matrix and found that energy efficiency, worker safety, and ethical sourcing were top priorities for both stakeholders and business impact. These became the focus of their ESG strategy.

## Section 3: Setting ESG Objectives

## Step 1: Turn Priorities into Objectives

Now that you know your top priorities, it’s time to set objectives. Good ESG objectives are:

- **Specific:** Clearly state what you want to achieve.
- **Measurable:** Include numbers or milestones.
- **Achievable:** Realistic given your resources.
- **Relevant:** Aligned with your priorities.
- **Time-bound:** Set a deadline.

> A well-crafted ESG objective is actionable, measurable, and directly linked to your organisation’s most material issues.

## Step 2: Examples of ESG Objectives

- **Environmental:** Reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 30% by 2030.
- **Social:** Increase the percentage of women in management roles to 40% within three years.
- **Governance:** Implement annual board diversity training for all directors by next fiscal year.

## Real-life example

A logistics company set an objective to electrify 50% of its fleet by 2028, directly supporting its strategic goal of becoming a leader in sustainable transportation. A bank set a goal to provide financial literacy training to 10,000 underserved individuals within two years.

## Step 3: Align Objectives with Strategy

Make sure your ESG objectives support your organisation’s overall mission and long-term strategy. This ensures buy-in from leadership and integration into business planning.

## Reflection question

Which ESG objective would create the most value for your organisation and its stakeholders if achieved?

## Step 4: Assign Responsibility and Resources

For each objective, decide:

- Who is responsible for achieving it?
- What resources, such as budget, staff, or technology, are needed?
- How will progress be tracked?

**Tip:** Assign clear ownership to ensure accountability. Regular check-ins help keep objectives on track.

## Section 4: Monitoring and Communicating Progress

Setting objectives is just the beginning. You’ll need to:

- Assign responsibility for each objective.
- Track progress with clear metrics.
- Communicate results, both successes and setbacks, to stakeholders.
- Adjust objectives as needed based on feedback and changing circumstances.

Transparency builds trust. Even if you fall short, honest reporting shows commitment and helps you learn for next time.

## Real-life example

A global food company publishes an annual sustainability report, sharing progress on ESG objectives, challenges faced, and next steps. This openness builds credibility with investors and customers.

## Reflection question

How will you communicate your ESG progress to your stakeholders? What channels — reports, meetings, website — will you use?

## Conclusions: Overcoming Common Challenges

## Challenge 1: Conflicting Priorities

Sometimes, business goals and ESG objectives can seem at odds. For example, reducing costs might conflict with investing in greener technologies. The key is to find win-win solutions, like energy efficiency projects that save money and reduce emissions.

## Challenge 2: Data Collection and Measurement

Measuring ESG progress can be tricky. Start with what you can measure and improve your data systems over time. Use industry benchmarks to compare your performance.

## Challenge 3: Keeping Momentum

ESG is a journey, not a one-time project. Celebrate small wins, share stories of impact, and keep ESG on the agenda at all levels of your organisation.

## Key Takeaways

- You can’t address every ESG issue at once, prioritisation is essential.
- Engage stakeholders and assess business impact to identify top priorities.
- Set clear, measurable ESG objectives aligned with your strategy.
- Assign responsibility and track progress.
- Communicate openly to build credibility and trust.
- Overcome challenges by staying flexible and committed.

## Quick Self-Check

- Have you identified your organisation’s most material ESG issues?
- Are your objectives specific, measurable, and aligned with your strategy?
- Do you have a plan to track and report progress?
- Are responsibilities and resources clearly assigned?
